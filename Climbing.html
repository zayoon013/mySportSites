Ancient Origins

Rock climbing has been practiced for thousands of years, with evidence of climbing in ancient China, Egypt, and among Native American tribes for practical purposes!

Olympic Debut

Sport climbing made its Olympic debut at the 2020 Tokyo Olympics, featuring speed climbing, bouldering, and lead climbing combined!

Everest Statistics

Mount Everest has been successfully climbed over 11,000 times by more than 6,000 different people since 1953!

Free Solo Legend

Alex Honnold's free solo climb of El Capitan in 2017 took 3 hours and 56 minutes to complete 3,000 feet of vertical granite without ropes!

Elite climbers can hang from a 20mm edge with just their fingertips, generating forces up to 3 times their body weight!

Mental Game

Studies show that climbing improves problem-solving skills, spatial awareness, and helps reduce anxiety and depression!

  • 5.5-5.7: Intermediate routes
  • 5.8-5.10: Advanced routes
  • 5.11-5.15: Expert to elite routes

V-Scale (Bouldering)

  • V0-V2: Beginner problems
  • V3-V5: Intermediate problems
  • V6-V9: Advanced problems
  • V10+: Elite level problems

üí° Pro Climbing Tips

  • Start with a Guide: Take a climbing course or hire a guide to learn proper safety techniques and fundamentals.
  • Use Your Legs: Climbing is about leg strength and technique, not just upper body power.
  • Trust Your Feet: Learn to trust your climbing shoes on small holds and steep terrain.
  • Read the Route: Study the climb before starting to plan your sequence and identify rest positions.
  • Practice Falling: Learning to fall safely is crucial for pushing your limits and improving.
  • Build Gradually: Progress slowly through grades to build strength and technique safely.
  • Rest and Recover: Climbing is hard on fingers and forearms - allow adequate recovery time.
  • Mental Game: Develop focus and overcome fear through visualization and controlled exposure.

üîß Climbing Techniques

Crimping

Gripping small holds with fingertips curved and thumb over fingers.

Mantling

Pressing up and over a ledge, similar to getting out of a swimming pool.

Stemming

Using opposing pressure between hands and feet in corners or chimneys.

Flagging

Using a free limb for balance without supporting body weight.
